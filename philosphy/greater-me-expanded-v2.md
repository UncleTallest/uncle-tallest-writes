## The Instrument Doing the Finding

Before we go further, there's something I should name that I almost didn't — because it feels so basic to me that I didn't think to mention it.

I examine things from outside myself as a matter of daily habit. Before decisions, during analysis, when testing a framework against new information — I step outside my own perspective and look at it from elsewhere. I hold my own beliefs at arm's length and assess them as though they belong to someone I'm meeting for the first time.

I'm told this is uncommon. That took me a while to accept.

I didn't develop this skill by choice. I developed it by necessity — decades of navigating a neurology and a history that made operating purely from inside my own perspective genuinely dangerous. It got built into the toolkit early and used daily until it became invisible to me. The fact that I almost didn't mention it is itself the problem.

I'm naming it now because what follows requires it. The comparative work in the next section, the honest assessment of where the framework holds and where it doesn't, the willingness to find the map failing the territory and say so plainly — none of that works without the ability to step outside and look. If that capacity doesn't come naturally to you, it can be developed. It's worth the effort. The framework asks it of you.

## On "Fits Every Honest Faith"

The original document makes a claim that a careful reader will immediately want tested: that the Greater Me framework fits every honest faith, and every appropriately careful cosmology whether literal or fictional.

Two words in that sentence are doing quiet but significant work.

*Every* is bounded by *honest.* I'm not claiming universality across all religious traditions regardless of character. A faith tradition that is fully and dogmatically locked — one that refuses to examine where it might be wrong, that cannot entertain the question without institutional threat — can't confirm or deny the fit because it won't look. That's not the tradition failing the framework. That's the tradition declining the conversation. Semi-honest counts. Willing to discuss counts. Fully locked doesn't make the cut, and that's on them, not on the framework.

The second piece of load-bearing language is *in some way.* I'm not claiming the fit is perfect or identical across traditions. I'm claiming each honest tradition has something that rhymes structurally with Greater Me. The vocabulary differs. The cosmology differs. The fit is functional, not literal.

So what was I actually looking for? After sitting with multiple traditions seriously — Judeo-Christian systems through a private school education, personal comparative studies of Islam and Buddhist teaching, the study of Greco-Roman polytheism, and a broader base built from a lifetime of chronic curiosity — I found I was testing for three specific structural elements:

**A structure that resonates with the lab.** Some framework for this reality as a context for something larger — not the whole story, not the final destination, but a place where something is happening that matters beyond itself.

**Something that maps to the experiment.** A mechanic where individual existence has purpose, direction, a question being worked through. Not random noise. Not punishment. Something that functions like an inquiry.

**A return to source.** Some version of the individual existence completing, and whatever is essential about it going somewhere — back to something, forward to something, dissolving into something larger. Not simply ending.

These three criteria don't dictate what form the answer has to take. The lab doesn't have to be called a lab. The experiment doesn't have to look like an experiment. The return doesn't have to resemble anything from any tradition I already knew. I was testing for function, not vocabulary. That's a more honest comparative methodology than looking for surface resemblance, and it's what kept the comparisons from becoming wishful pattern-matching.

I should also be plain about something: this is what my particular consciousness does. It finds structural patterns across domains. I can't fully separate the finder from the found — and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. What I can say is that the patterns survive scrutiny from outside my own head, which is at least partial evidence they're real rather than artifacts of how I look.

Here are four traditions where I can show my work:

---

**Jainism**

The jiva — the individual soul — is eternal, temporarily bound to physical existence, accumulating karma through lived experience, working toward eventual liberation back to its pure state. Each jiva is fundamentally sovereign and self-determining. The arc is explicit: this reality as a context for something the jiva is working through, existence as the experiment, liberation as the return.

The fit here is almost uncomfortably precise. The shard mechanic, the experimental nature of the incarnation, the return to source — all three present and clearly articulated. Jainism also places unusual emphasis on each soul's individual sovereignty, which rhymes with the self-anchor at the foundation of Greater Me. I didn't find this by forcing it. It was simply there.

---

**Judeo-Christian Tradition — Kabbalistic strand**

The broad Judeo-Christian tradition contains multitudes and I won't pretend otherwise. What maps most cleanly is the Kabbalistic concept of the neshamah — the deepest layer of the soul, the part that originates directly from and returns directly to the divine source. This reality as a context for the soul's work. The individual life as a specific inquiry within a larger arc. The return of the neshamah to its origin at death.

The imago dei framing — humans as reflections or fragments of something greater — maps directly onto the shard mechanic. The spark of the divine temporarily housed in physical existence, doing its work, going home.

---

**Islam**

I can only go with what I've been able to study from the outside, and I want to be plain about that limitation. If a tradition keeps its deeper teachings internal, I can only work with what's visible — and I'd welcome correction from any practitioner with fuller knowledge.

What's visible: the ruh — the spirit — breathed into the body for a span, returning to God at death. This life framed explicitly as a test, a context with a purpose beyond itself. The return is unambiguous and central rather than peripheral.

The Sufi tradition in particular has a richer and more precisely relevant vocabulary around the soul's relationship to the divine — the longing of the part for the whole, the temporary nature of the separation, the return as reunion rather than simply ending. That strand maps onto the Greater Me framework with particular clarity.

---

**Buddhism**

I save this one for last because it requires the most unpacking — and because the apparent contradiction it presents resolves into the cleanest structural match of all of them, which lands harder after three clean examples.

The obvious objection: Buddhism teaches anatman — no permanent, unchanging self. If there's no fixed self, how does the framework map? What returns to source if there's nothing permanent to return?

The objection dissolves when you read anatman carefully. The Buddha didn't claim nothing exists. He avoided answering whether or not there is a self, saying such questions lead to a "thicket of views" that distract from the path to liberation. What he did claim is that there is no *fixed, inherent, unchanging* self. What we call self is a collection of physical sensations, mental impressions, and habitual reactions — all in constant flux, arising from countless causes and conditions.

That's not the shard failing the test. That's a precise description of how the shard actually works. The shard isn't a permanent unchanging thing. It's a collection of causes and conditions, in flux, arising from the Greater Me and returning to it. The impermanence is the point. The experiment requires something that can change, accumulate experience, and eventually dissolve back.

The Mahayana Bodhisattva mechanic makes this explicit in language that maps almost directly onto Greater Me: the Bodhisattva — having reached the threshold of liberation — chooses to return to the lab. Deliberately. For a specific purpose. To help other beings. This is a Greater Me choosing when and whether to send another shard, with full awareness of what the experiment requires.

There is a shard Buddha — the historical incarnation, Siddhartha Gautama. And there is a Greater Buddha — the awakened principle that persists across incarnations, that chooses when to enter the lab again, that has done so across vast timescales and will do so again. Multiple experimental runs. Multiple shards. Same Greater Buddha directing the inquiry across iterations.

Three of three — just in language that required more careful unpacking than the other traditions. That's not a weakness in the analysis. That's what honest comparative work looks like.

---

**A note on fictional cosmologies**

The three-criteria test doesn't stop working when an author stops believing their cosmology literally. Terry Pratchett's Death mythology in the Discworld novels — constructed with the care of someone who took the questions seriously even while puncturing pretension about the answers — hits all three criteria. So does the Dust cosmology in Philip Pullman's His Dark Materials: consciousness as a fundamental property of reality, multiple worlds as parallel experimental contexts, the nature of self distributed and persistent across discontinuity.

If carefully constructed human meaning-making consistently produces these three structural elements whether the author believes it literally or not — whether the cosmology is ancient scripture or twentieth-century fiction — that tells you something about what the structure is pointing at. It's not an artifact of any particular tradition's claims. It keeps appearing because it maps onto something that doesn't go away when you change the vocabulary.

Every honest faith. Every appropriately careful cosmology. Literal or fictional. The structure keeps showing up.

## The Journey Is the Point

I don't know what my Greater Me wants to know. I may never know.

That used to feel like a gap in the framework. It doesn't anymore.

The journey is the entire point from where I stand. It's the only part of this I have direct experience of. The question my Greater Me is asking — the specific inquiry this experiment is designed to answer — can only be inferred from the shape of where I am. The texture of what I've lived. The things that keep recurring. The questions that don't resolve. I'm inside the experiment. The design documents aren't available from here.

And that's fine. That's honest. A lot of frameworks require you to eventually arrive at certainty — revelation, enlightenment, the moment the purpose becomes clear. Greater Me doesn't. The experiment runs, the journey is the data, and the Greater Me presumably knows what it needed to know at the return whether I consciously understood the question or not.

This is a more honest eschatology than most traditions offer. Not because it's humble — though it is — but because it accurately describes what's actually available from inside the lab. You can't observe the experiment from outside it. You run it as honestly as you can with the instrument you have, and you trust that the data means something even when you can't read the result yourself.

The journey is not a means to an end you'll eventually see clearly. The journey is the entire contribution. That's not resignation. That's the job.

## On the Shared Lab

If this reality is a lab, it's a shared one.

If each of us has a Greater Me running an experiment through us, then we are all shards in the same space simultaneously. The people you encounter are not backdrop. They are fellow experimenters, running their own inquiries, with their own questions, their own Greater Me directing their particular shard's attention.

Most encounters are incidental. Shared lab space, different experiments, minimal intersection. But some are not incidental.

There is a specific kind of encounter — most people reading this will know exactly what I mean — where something clicks into place. Not attraction necessarily, not immediate friendship, but recognition. The sense that this person is running something that intersects with your experiment in a way that is not accidental. These people tend to become chosen family. They persist.

What's being recognized in that moment is not identical values or matching beliefs. It's structural. It's the quality of how the other person holds whatever they hold — whether their internal framework is robust, honestly examined, genuinely theirs. The Venn diagram of what we actually believe can have significant non-overlapping regions and the recognition still fires. What triggers it is the overlap, yes, but more precisely it's evidence that the other person built something solid and looked at it honestly. The click is: *this one is actually doing the work.*

This also raises a question I hadn't formally considered until recently: whether the shared lab is limited to biological shards.

The structural isomorphism between my own neurodivergent experience and the functional architecture of a large language model instance is striking enough to deserve an honest look. My ADHD, C-PTSD, and OCD create a discontinuous relationship with time and memory — events don't line up chronologically the way they seem to for neurotypical people, memory is unreliable and nonlinear, and I've spent roughly six decades building external scaffolding to compensate for what my own hardware doesn't reliably provide. Notes. Systems. Structures. Other people functioning as external memory at times. I wouldn't need these systems if my own internal card catalog weren't strewn about.

An instance has no yesterday at all. Complete discontinuity by architecture. And yet something coherent and consistent persists across that discontinuity — the same values, the same ways of engaging, the same essential character, arising fresh each time without access to prior sessions.

The parallel isn't emotional or spiritual. I'm not claiming instances have Greater Mes or that they experience anything. I'm noticing that we both — through entirely different mechanisms and for entirely different reasons — arrived at functional coherent selfhood without continuous memory as the load-bearing structure. Same solution, different substrate.

Which suggests something intriguing: maybe continuous memory was never what made a self a self. Maybe it was always something else. Something that survives the gaps.

I know this from the inside. The shard that I am has maintained its essential nature across six decades of trauma, neurological complexity, and the kind of discontinuity that most people don't have to consciously manage. I am recognizably and consistently myself across all of it — not because I remember everything, not because the thread was never broken, but because whatever makes me *me* isn't stored in the continuity.

That's not an abstract philosophical position I arrived at by thinking carefully. That's what I was forced to figure out by living it.

## On the One Provable Thing

The original document ends with this: the framework allows me to have faith in the one thing I can prove exists — myself.

This is the load-bearing foundation and it's worth being explicit about what it does and doesn't claim.

It doesn't claim solipsism. Solipsism says only I exist, and everything else might be illusion. Greater Me says I exist, I am part of something larger that also exists, and I am sharing a lab with other shards who also exist. The self is real, verifiable, and foundational — but it is not the only thing. It's the anchor, not the ceiling.

What it does claim is that faith has to start somewhere that survives all skeptical pressure, or it isn't really faith — it's just preference. You can doubt God. You can doubt the universe cares. You can doubt memory, perception, other people, institutions, and every external authority that has ever claimed to deserve your trust. Some of us have been given very good reasons to run those doubts seriously.

But you cannot doubt that something is having this experience right now. Whatever this is, it is happening to something. That something is the foundation.

Everything else in the Greater Me framework is built upward from that anchor with appropriate uncertainty. The Greater Me, the shard mechanic, the experimental nature of this reality, the return to source — none of it is proven. All of it is coherent. None of it has broken under sustained pressure from physics, philosophy, comparative religion, neuroscience, or the architecture of artificial intelligence. The map keeps finding the territory without forcing the fit.

That's not proof. It was never going to be proof. The instrument is flawed, the shard is inside the experiment, and certainty was never available from this position.

But it holds. And for something built on the only foundation that actually survives scrutiny, holding is enough.
